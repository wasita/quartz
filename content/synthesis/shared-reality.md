# shared reality

- perceived commonality of inner states (feelings, beliefs, concerns) with another person about something ([[2021-higgins-shared-reality|Higgins, 2021]])
- dual-motive construct
    1. relational — need to connect
    2. epistemic — need to understand the world

    - not independent of each other; synergistic
    - rejects treating cognition and connection as separable
    - sharing inner states with others simultaneously makes those states feel more objectively true _and_ deepens social bonds

## defining features

- four criteria that distinguish shared reality from related constructs ([[2009-echterhoff-shared-reality-experiencing|Echterhoff et al., 2009]]):
    1. **subjective commonality** — must be _experienced_ as shared, not merely objectively overlapping
    2. **specific referent** — about something in the world (a target, event, situation)
    3. **motivational engagement** — requires active motivation to connect, not passive co-occurrence
    4. **experienced connection** — must involve felt epistemic or relational connection to the other person

## why it matters

- humans have a fundamental need to belong — a drive to form and maintain lasting positive relationships that shapes emotion, cognition, behavior, and health across cultures ([[1995-baumeister-need-belong-desire|Baumeister & Leary, 1995]])
- loneliness and social isolation independently predict premature mortality with effect sizes comparable to smoking and obesity ([[2015-holt-lunstad-loneliness-social-isolation|Holt-Lunstad et al., 2015]])
- poor social relationships associated with ~29% increased CHD risk and ~32% increased stroke risk ([[2016-valtorta-loneliness-social-isolation|Valtorta et al., 2016]])
- shared reality is a core mechanism through which social bonds are formed and sustained

# how shared reality is created

## audience-tuning / sharing-is-believing

- when people communicate about a target to an audience, they tune their message to fit the audience's attitude ([[2018-echterhoff-shared-reality-created|Echterhoff & Schmalbach, 2018]])
    - this tuning, crucially, shapes their own memory of the target
    - memory distortion only occurs when communicator is genuinely motivated to establish shared reality with audience
- shared reality created about one target can generalize to novel related targets — under conditions of high audience commonality, active recollection, and target similarity ([[2015-bebermeier-generalization-shared-reality|Bebermeier et al., 2015]])

## shared experience and coattention

- sharing an experience with another person — even without explicit communication — amplifies its subjective intensity ([[2014-boothby-shared-experiences-amplified|Boothby et al., 2014]])
    - both pleasant and unpleasant sensations rated more intensely in shared conditions
    - mechanism: coattention recruits greater attentional and cognitive resources
- collective attention functions as a signal of common knowledge and amplifies cognitive processing ([[2020-shteynberg-shared-worlds-shared|Shteynberg et al., 2020]])
- however, non-communicative online co-presence (e.g., passively co-watching video) fails to activate bonding mechanisms — equivalence tests showed no difference from solo-watching ([[2025-wolf-online-experience-sharing|Wolf & Dotson, 2025]])
- in-person co-watching with multimodal synchrony (facial expressions, EDA, character impressions) does predict social connection — these indicators load onto a latent shared-experience factor ([[2023-cheong-synchronized-affect-shared|Cheong et al., 2023]])

## self-disclosure

- intimate disclosers are liked more, people disclose more to liked others, and disclosing increases liking for the recipient — disclosure and liking form a dynamic, mutually reinforcing system ([[1994-collins-self-disclosure-liking-meta-analytic|Collins & Miller, 1994]])
- listeners report greater liking and closeness after one-way disclosure, but differences disappear after role reversal; perceived similarity partially mediates the listener advantage ([[2013-sprecher-effects-self-disclosure-role|Sprecher et al., 2013]])

## implicit social tuning

- affiliative social tuning: spontaneous alignment of implicit attitudes (measured via IAT) with an interaction partner's apparent views ([[2018-skorinko-shared-reality-through|Skorinko & Sinclair, 2018]])
    - implicit prejudice shifts to mirror partners' presumed attitudes when affiliative motivation is high
    - shared reality can form at levels below conscious awareness

## behavioral signals

- conversational response time is a simple, robust, and honest signal of social connection — faster inter-turn response times (particularly sub-250ms) predict greater felt connection and are readable by both participants and eavesdroppers ([[2022-templeton-fast-response-times|Templeton et al., 2022]])

# generalized shared reality (SR-G)

- measured using [self-report questionnaire](https://cuhigginslab.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/05/Generalized-Shared-Reality-SR-G-Rossignac-Milon.pdf)
- [[2021-rosssignac-merged-minds|Rossignac-Milon et al., 2021]]
- sense of sharing inner states with a partner about the world _in general_
- theoretically distinct from:
    - target-specific shared reality
    - perceived similarity
    - partner responsiveness
- predicts connection, rapport, epistemic uncertainty, desire to interact again, above and beyond all 3 above ^

# epistemic companionship

- close relationships are epistemic partnerships — partners jointly construct shared reality ([[2018-rossignac-milon-epistemic-companions-shared|Rossignac-Milon & Higgins, 2018]])
- shared reality in relationships progresses through four phases:
    1. shared feelings
    2. shared practices
    3. shared coordination
    4. shared identity
- partners become "epistemic companions" who collaboratively make sense of the world

# similarity, projection, and the self-as-anchor

- homophily: contact between similar people occurs at higher rates, confining social networks to similar others and shaping opportunities, health, and political views ([[2001-mcpherson-birds-feather-homophily|McPherson et al., 2001]])
- attraction is a positive linear function of the proportion of similar attitudes ([[1965-byrne-attraction-linear-function|Byrne & Nelson, 1965]])
- shared music preferences create interpersonal bonds through signaling value similarity (not personality similarity) — perceived value similarity mediates the link ([[2011-boer-shared-preferences-music|Boer et al., 2011]])
- music is the most frequent topic in get-acquainted conversations and carries valid personality information ([[2006-rentfrow-message-ballad-role|Rentfrow & Gosling, 2006]])

## cognitive mechanisms of person modeling

- people routinely use their own knowledge as a default anchor when constructing models of what others know, leading to systematic overestimation of shared knowledge — this unifies false consensus, hindsight bias, and curse of knowledge under knowledge imputation with insufficient adjustment ([[1999-nickerson-knowledge-imputation|Nickerson, 1999]])
- the false consensus effect: tendency to overestimate how widely others share one's own beliefs and behaviors ([[1977-ross-false-consensus-effect|Ross et al., 1977]])
- perceived similarity acts as a switch: people use projection (self as model) for similar targets and stereotyping (group membership) for dissimilar targets ([[2004-ames-projection-stereotyping|Ames, 2004]])
- social inference follows anchoring-and-adjustment from the self — greater self-other discrepancy predicts longer reaction times, consistent with serial adjustment from a self-anchor ([[2013-tamir-anchoring-adjustment-during|Tamir & Mitchell, 2013]])
- speakers show egocentric bias toward their own knowledge when formulating messages; message informativeness varies inversely with perceived addressee familiarity ([[1992-fussell-coordination-knowledge-communication|Fussell & Krauss, 1992]])

# communication and common ground

- communication depends on common ground; communities are the primary source ([[1996-clark-communities-commonalities-communication|Clark, 1996]])
    - language is a coordination device grounded in community-level shared knowledge
    - both communal (from community membership) and personal (from direct interaction) common ground are necessary
- common ground as complex propositional attitudes built iteratively from individual attitudes — the context of conversation is the set of propositions all participants mutually accept ([[2002-stalnaker-common-ground|Stalnaker, 2002]])
- CHAI model: meaning is jointly inferred across two nested timescales — partner-specific common ground and community-wide conventions ([[2023-hawkins-partners-populations-hierarchical|Hawkins et al., 2023]])
    - reconciles stable linguistic conventions with flexible partner-specific meaning-making

# scope: local vs. global shared reality

- psychological distance determines whether people align with local (specific individuals) or global (broader group norms) social influences ([[2018-ledgerwood-achieving-local-global|Ledgerwood & Wang, 2018]])
    - proximity → alignment with specific individuals
    - distance → alignment with group norms
- shared reality in politics: the desire to share reality is a motivational engine behind political attitude adoption and sustains conflicts through competing group-level representations ([[2018-stern-political-aspects-shared|Stern & Ondish, 2018]])

# developmental origins

- two-year-olds already identify shared preferences and use them to infer novel object choices — though generalization is category-specific ([[2010-fawcett-children-reason-shared|Fawcett & Markson, 2010]])

# computational approaches to social cognition

- BToM (bayesian theory of mind): models joint inference of beliefs, desires, and percepts from observed behavior — accurately captures quantitative human mental-state judgments ([[2017-baker-rational-quantitative-attribution|Baker et al., 2017]])
- emotion prediction as computation over a generative theory of mind: observers infer mental states via inverse planning, compute appraisals, then map to emotions; social preferences are essential and irreducible ([[2023-houlihan-emotion-prediction-computation|Houlihan et al., 2023]])
- social-structure learning: people infer latent group membership from observed behavior via bayesian inference, going beyond dyadic similarity ([[2020-gershman-social-structure-learning|Gershman & Cikara, 2020]]; [[2017-gershman-learning-structure-social|Gershman et al., 2017]])
- person knowledge acquisition integrates face/voice recognition with person-specific trait and mental-state inference through cascading representations ([[2020-anzellotti-acquisition-person-knowledge|Anzellotti & Young, 2020]])

# key paradigm shifts

- psychology has been limited by focus on isolated individuals; the most important determinants of development, cognition, and mental health emerge from social interaction — a science of interacting minds requires new paradigms and tools ([[2024-wheatley-emerging-science-interacting|Wheatley et al., 2024]])

# open questions

- how does shared reality interact with computational models of social cognition? can bayesian models of theory of mind (e.g., BToM) be extended to capture the _creation_ of shared reality through iterative communication?
- what role does recursive mentalizing play in establishing shared reality (i.e., the process of establishing shared reality through conversation)?
- what distinguishes shared reality from mere common knowledge or common ground computationally?
- how does the self-as-anchor bias interact with shared reality formation — does anchoring on one's own states facilitate or hinder genuine shared reality?
- can the generalization of shared reality (from one target to novel targets) be modeled as hierarchical bayesian inference?
