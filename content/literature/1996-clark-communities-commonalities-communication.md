# summary

>[!example] key terms
> common ground, communities of practice, communal lexicon, grounding, shared knowledge, linguistic relativity, coordination

clark argues that communication depends on common ground and that communities are the primary source. the chapter distinguishes communal common ground (from community membership) from personal common ground (from direct interaction) and treats language as a coordination device grounded in community-level shared knowledge.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

how people communicate despite non-identical knowledge is a fundamental question with implications for linguistic relativity, meaning, and cross-cultural communication.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

clark's grounding theory (clark & schaefer 1989; clark & brennan 1991) and grice's cooperative principle. situated within the whorf-sapir linguistic relativity debate.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

psycholinguistic studies of reference, lexical choice, and dialogue. clark's experimental work on collaborative reference. systematic evidence for community-level common ground effects is relatively sparse.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

communities provide communal common ground enabling language use. language is a coordination device: words are conventional solutions to coordination problems within communities. both communal and personal common ground are necessary.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

theoretical/review paper; no empirical hypotheses.
