# summary

>[!example] key terms
> self-disclosure, liking, closeness, get-acquainted, discloser, listener, perceived similarity, role effects

sprecher et al. examine whether discloser vs. listener role differentially affects liking. listeners report greater liking and closeness after initial one-way disclosure, but differences disappear after role reversal.

full citation: [@sprecher2013effects]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

most disclosure research treats disclosure as mutual. examining role asymmetry has implications for relationship initiation.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

social penetration theory predicts disclosure promotes closeness for both. uncertainty reduction theory suggests listeners gain more closeness. the paper tests role asymmetry, a gap in prior work.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

mutual disclosure generates closeness. aron et al.'s fast friends procedure shows structured disclosure creates closeness rapidly. role asymmetry had not been tested.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

the listener role confers greater immediate liking and closeness because listening creates perceived similarity and positive impressions.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) listeners report greater liking. (2) listeners feel greater closeness. (3) differences diminish after role reversal. (4) perceived similarity mediates.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

laboratory experiment with unacquainted pairs; two-interaction design with role reversal.

> ***what data is used?***

undergraduate dyads; self-reported liking, closeness, perceived similarity after each interaction.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

structured self-disclosure task; within- and between-person comparisons; mediation analyses.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

listeners reported more liking, closeness, and perceived similarity initially. after role reversal, asymmetries disappeared. perceived similarity partially mediated.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

role asymmetry matters early but resolves with reciprocity.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

being the listener confers short-term advantages, mediated by perceived similarity. asymmetries resolve once both parties disclose.
