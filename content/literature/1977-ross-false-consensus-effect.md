# summary

>[!example] key terms
> false consensus effect, egocentric bias, social projection, attribution theory, dispositional inference, consensus estimation

Ross, Greene, and House introduced and empirically demonstrated the "false consensus effect" — the tendency for people to overestimate how widely others share their own beliefs, choices, and behaviors. across four studies, they showed that individuals perceive their own responses as the modal response and make more extreme dispositional inferences about those who respond differently.

full citation: [@ross1977false]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

do people systematically overestimate how much others agree with them? this is important because social judgments underlie cooperation, conflict, and moral evaluation — if consensus estimates are systematically biased by one's own perspective, social perception is distorted at its core.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

attribution theory (Heider; Jones & Davis) examined how observers infer dispositions from behavior but supplied participants with consensus information rather than measuring natural estimates. the "attributive projection" literature described tendencies to project traits onto others, often framed in psychodynamic terms.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

Katz and Allport (1931) provided early evidence that students' estimates of how many others cheat correlated with their own cheating behavior. however, prior attribution research provided consensus information rather than measuring what consensus people spontaneously assume.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

people use their own behavioral choices as an anchor when estimating prevalence in the general population, leading to systematic overestimation of consensus.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) people will perceive their own response as more common and estimate a majority of others would respond as they do. (2) people who choose differently from the observer will receive more extreme personality trait attributions.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

four experimental studies combining questionnaire-based hypothetical scenarios and a real-world behavioral paradigm.

> ***what data is used?***

participant responses to hypothetical dilemmas and a real behavioral choice (whether to walk around campus wearing a sandwich board sign saying "eat at Joe's").

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

participants made a choice, then estimated the percentage of others who would make the same choice, and rated the personality traits of people who chose each option.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

participants consistently overestimated agreement with their own choices. those who agreed to wear the sign estimated 62% of others would also agree; those who refused estimated only 33%. furthermore, those who chose differently were judged as having more extreme or unusual personalities.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

both hypotheses were confirmed across all studies. the false consensus effect is robust across hypothetical and real behavioral contexts.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

the false consensus effect is a pervasive egocentric bias. people treat their own responses as natural and normative while viewing alternative responses as reflecting stable, deviant personal characteristics. proposed mechanisms include selective exposure, cognitive availability of one's own perspective, and motivated reasoning.
