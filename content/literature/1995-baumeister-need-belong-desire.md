# summary

>[!example] key terms
> belongingness, social attachment, fundamental motivation, interpersonal relationships, affiliation, social exclusion, evolutionary psychology

baumeister and leary propose that humans have a fundamental need to belong - a drive to form and maintain lasting, positive relationships. they review broad evidence showing this need shapes emotion, cognition, behavior, and health.

full citation: [@baumeister1995]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

understanding why humans seek social connection has implications for motivation, emotion, health, and psychopathology. establishing belonging as fundamental reorganizes how social behavior should be studied.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior theories treated affiliation as secondary. the paper synthesizes attachment theory (bowlby), humanistic motivation theory (maslow), and evolutionary perspectives, arguing each alone is insufficient.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

evidence from loneliness, ostracism, bereavement, group membership, and social support studies pointed to harmful effects of deprivation but had not been unified under one construct.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

belonging is a fundamental motivation with two components: (1) frequent nonaversive interaction and (2) stable caring relational bonds. the drive is innate, universal, and produces pervasive effects.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

if fundamental: (1) people seek bonds and resist dissolution; (2) satiation but not easy substitution; (3) deprivation causes negative affect and health consequences; (4) cross-cultural universality.

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

people form attachments readily across cultures; dissolution causes strong distress; pervasive effects on emotion, cognition, and health; satiation effects occur; social exclusion impairs cognitive function.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

the breadth supports belonging as a genuine fundamental motivation that cannot be reduced to other motivations.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

the need to belong is a powerful, fundamental, universal human motivation essential for well-being.
