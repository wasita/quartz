# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared reality, social tuning, implicit prejudice, affiliative motivation, IAT, attitude alignment, intergroup attitudes

skorinko and sinclair review how affiliative social tuning - the spontaneous alignment of implicit attitudes with an interaction partner's apparent views when one desires to get along - creates shared reality at the level of implicit prejudice. implicit prejudice measured via the IAT adjusts to mirror a partner's presumed attitudes when affiliative motivation is high.

full citation: [@skorinko2018shared]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

implicit prejudice is typically conceived as stable. establishing that it fluctuates in response to interpersonal motivation challenges this view and has implications for prejudice reduction.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

shared reality theory provides the overarching framework. affiliative social tuning builds on sinclair et al.'s epistemic tuning work and self-categorization theory.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior IAT research showed implicit prejudice is malleable under some conditions. this paper extends the review to implicit measures.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

shared reality operates at the implicit level: affiliative motivation drives genuine alignment of implicit prejudice with a partner's perceived views.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

key propositions reviewed: (1) high affiliative motivation reduces implicit prejudice when the partner appears low-prejudice; (2) collectivist orientation amplifies social tuning; (3) effects reflect genuine attitude change.

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

affiliative social tuning of implicit prejudice is genuine: when people are motivated to get along, their implicit attitudes shift toward their partner's, creating shared intergroup realities.
