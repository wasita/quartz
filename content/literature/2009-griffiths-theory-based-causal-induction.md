# summary

>[!example] key terms
> causal induction, bayesian inference, causal theory, ontology, functional form, structure learning, domain-specific knowledge

griffiths and tenenbaum propose a hierarchical bayesian framework for causal induction where domain-general inference is guided by domain-specific prior knowledge encoded in abstract causal theories. three components of prior knowledge - ontology, plausibility, functional form - are demonstrated across causal learning paradigms.

full citation: [@griffiths2009theory]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

causal induction is foundational to cognition, enabling prediction, control, and explanation. understanding how people learn causal structure from limited data is central to cognitive science.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

causal graphical models (pearl), power PC theory (cheng), associationist models (rescorla-wagner). most prior accounts are domain-general and don't explain how background knowledge shapes inference.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

work on contingency learning, causal power estimation, and structure learning. prior models captured narrow paradigms and failed to generalize.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

causal induction is bayesian inference over a hypothesis space shaped by abstract causal theories specifying ontology, plausible relations, and functional forms.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

human judgments will be sensitive to domain-specific constraints; learning will be faster when priors match true structure; the same bayesian machinery generalizes across domains.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

theoretical paper with computational modeling applied to existing empirical datasets.

> ***what data is used?***

existing experimental datasets from causal learning studies; model predictions compared to human judgments.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

hierarchical bayesian modeling; graphical causal models; model comparison against alternatives; goodness-of-fit analyses.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

the theory-based framework accounts for human causal judgments better than alternatives across a wide range of paradigms.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

domain-specific prior knowledge is essential; purely domain-general accounts are insufficient.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

causal induction reflects hierarchical bayesian inference with abstract causal theories as structured priors, unifying disparate findings.
