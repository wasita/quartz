# summary

>[!example] key terms
> homophily, social networks, similarity, network ties, segregation, network structure, social inequality

mcpherson, smith-lovin, and cook conduct a comprehensive review of homophily - the principle that contact between similar people occurs at a higher rate than among dissimilar people. they distinguish baseline (structural) from inbreeding (choice) homophily and catalog the dimensions on which it operates.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

social network structure determines the flow of information, support, and influence. because homophily confines contacts to similar others, it shapes opportunities, health, political views, and social mobility.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

balance theory, social comparison theory, and contact hypothesis each predict aspects of homophily. the authors distinguish structural from choice homophily. teasing these apart empirically is difficult.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

decades of research on marriage, friendship, and organizational membership show strong homophily on race, ethnicity, age, religion, education, occupation, and gender. less evidence on dynamic processes.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

homophily is a pervasive, multi-dimensional structural property of social networks with cumulative consequences for social inequality and isolation.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

key generalizations: (1) race and ethnicity produce strongest homophily; (2) age, religion, education follow; (3) dissimilar ties dissolve faster; (4) structural opportunity shapes homophily as much as preference.
