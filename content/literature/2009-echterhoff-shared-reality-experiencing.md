# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared reality, inner states, saying-is-believing, epistemic needs, relational needs, audience tuning, common ground

echterhoff, higgins, and levine introduce a systematic conceptualization of shared reality as the experience of commonality in inner states about a target with another person or group. they outline four defining features, distinguish shared reality from related constructs (common ground, empathy, theory of mind), and review evidence from the saying-is-believing paradigm.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

humans have a fundamental need to validate their inner states by experiencing that others share them. clarifying the construct of shared reality and its motivational basis addresses a core question about why people are so strongly motivated to achieve interpersonal consensus about their subjective experiences.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

draws on festinger's social comparison theory, higgins's shared reality theory and regulatory focus, clark's common ground framework, and research on empathy and theory of mind. argues these prior frameworks either miss the subjective inner-state component or the motivational underpinnings of shared reality.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

the saying-is-believing paradigm (higgins & rholes 1978) showed communicators shift their memory and evaluations toward the audience's attitude when they experience shared reality. existing literature lacked a unified construct definition distinguishing shared reality from mere behavioral coordination or mutual knowledge.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

shared reality is a distinct psychological construct defined by: (a) subjective commonality of inner states (not just behaviors), (b) about a specific referent, (c) with appropriate motivational engagement, and (d) involving experienced connection to another's inner states. it serves fundamental epistemic needs (reliable knowledge) and relational needs (connectedness).

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

the framework implies: (1) shared reality is experienced when communicators successfully tune messages to an audience's attitude and receive signals of epistemic connection. (2) shared reality motivates memory and evaluative shifts toward the audience's position. (3) both epistemic and relational motives independently drive shared-reality creation.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

theoretical/review paper with synthesis of empirical studies (primarily saying-is-believing paradigm experiments).

> ***what data is used?***

review of existing experimental studies; no new primary data collected.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

conceptual analysis, construct definition, systematic review of saying-is-believing and audience-tuning experiments.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

n/a (review paper, but documents that shared reality effects reliably appear in saying-is-believing paradigm studies).

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

the conceptualization clarifies what shared reality is and is not, distinguishing it from common ground (cognitive/behavioral rather than experiential) and empathy. the four-component definition provides a framework for designing future empirical tests.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

shared reality is a fundamental human experience with distinct epistemic and relational motivational bases, and the saying-is-believing paradigm provides the key empirical window into its creation and consequences for memory, evaluation, and social behavior.
