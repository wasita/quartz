# summary

>[!example] key terms
> projection, stereotyping, mental state inference, perceived similarity, social judgment, mentalizing, person perception

ames proposes and tests a similarity-contingency model: perceivers use projection (self as model) for similar targets and stereotyping (group membership) for dissimilar targets. perceived similarity acts as a switch. three experiments support the model.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

inferring others' mental states is central to social life. understanding the cognitive mechanisms has broad implications for social psychology and intergroup relations.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

simulation theory (projection), theory-theory (stereotyping), and dual-process accounts. prior work treated them separately; ames integrates them. no prior work specified the trigger for each strategy.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

people project onto similar others and stereotype dissimilar others, but the moderating role of perceived general similarity was untested. limited process-level evidence existed.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

similarity functions as a switch: similar targets trigger projection, dissimilar targets trigger stereotyping.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) similarity increases projection and decreases stereotyping. (2) reaction times reflect the serial process of accessing self vs. category knowledge.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

three experiments with between-subjects manipulations of perceived similarity; self-report and reaction time measures.

> ***what data is used?***

attitude judgments, mental state attributions, and reaction times across three studies.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

experimental manipulations of similarity; measurement of projection and stereotyping; reaction time analyses.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

similarity condition increased projection and decreased stereotyping; reaction times were consistent with the predicted strategy.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

perceived similarity is a key moderator of inferential strategy.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

mental state inference involves projection and stereotyping, flexibly deployed based on perceived similarity.
