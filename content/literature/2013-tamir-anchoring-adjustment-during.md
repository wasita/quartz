# summary

>[!example] key terms
> anchoring and adjustment, self-other overlap, mentalizing, social inferences, simulation theory, reaction time, egocentric bias

tamir and mitchell test whether social inference follows anchoring-and-adjustment from the self. greater self-other discrepancy predicts longer reaction times for similar but not dissimilar targets, consistent with serial adjustment from a self-anchor.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

simulation theories predict self-knowledge grounds understanding of similar others, but the specific mechanism was poorly specified. identifying it has implications for understanding social understanding failures.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

simulation theory, anchoring-and-adjustment heuristic (tversky & kahneman). no prior work tested serial adjustment in social inference using RT methods.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

fMRI work showed differential activation when mentalizing about similar vs. dissimilar others. behavioral process data were lacking.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

the self functions as a cognitive anchor during inference about similar others; perceivers serially adjust away from it. this is specific to similar targets.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) self-other discrepancy predicts longer RTs for similar targets. (2) no such relationship for dissimilar targets.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

three behavioral experiments; participants judged attitudes of similar or dissimilar targets and reported their own; reaction times were the primary measure.

> ***what data is used?***

attitude ratings and reaction times from laboratory samples across three studies.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

within- and between-subjects designs; regression of RT on self-other discrepancy magnitude.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

consistent positive linear relationship between discrepancy and RT for similar targets; no relationship for dissimilar targets.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

process-level evidence for simulation theory: the self is the starting point and adjustment is effortful, explaining egocentric biases.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

anchoring-and-adjustment from self is the mechanism for understanding similar others, explaining both adaptive use and systematic failures of self-knowledge.
