# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared reality, political attitudes, polarization, social influence, political conflict, intergroup relations, attitude formation

stern and ondish review how the pursuit, creation, and maintenance of shared reality underlies important political phenomena. they argue that the desire to share reality shapes political attitudes, that competing representations sustain political conflict, and that shared reality contributes to both social stasis and change.

full citation: [@stern2018political]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

understanding why people hold the political attitudes they do, why political conflicts persist, and how societies change or remain stable are fundamental questions. shared reality provides a motivational framework for addressing all three.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

shared reality theory (higgins) posits that people are motivated to perceive their inner states as valid and shared with others. related work includes social comparison theory and motivated reasoning.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior empirical work shows that communicating about events leads people to adopt the audience's perspective (saying-is-believing), and that political attitudes cluster within social networks. the evidence base is relatively lab-based.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

shared reality - constructed and maintained through governments, political groups, and close others - is a primary motivational engine behind political attitude adoption, the persistence of political conflict, and the pace of social change.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

no formal hypotheses tested (theoretical review). implied: (1) affiliative motivation toward a political group shifts attitudes toward that group's reality; (2) competing realities explain resistant conflicts; (3) dominant representations constrain social change.
