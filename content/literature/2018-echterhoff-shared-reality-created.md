# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared reality, audience tuning, saying-is-believing, epistemic needs, interpersonal communication, message adaptation, memory distortion

echterhoff and schmalbach review how shared reality is constructed through interpersonal communication, focusing on the audience-tuning process in which speakers adapt their messages to match an audience's attitudes. they detail the conditions — particularly epistemic and relational motivations — under which this tuning produces genuine shared-reality experience that shapes communicators' own memories and evaluations.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

communication is the primary arena in which shared reality is forged, yet the micro-level processes by which a speaker's message adaptation generates shared-reality experience were not fully integrated. understanding these processes clarifies how interpersonal communication shapes belief, memory, and social knowledge.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

builds directly on echterhoff, higgins & levine (2009) shared reality framework and higgins & rholes (1978) saying-is-believing effect. engages with gricean pragmatics and clark's common ground, extending these to explain when and why message tuning produces phenomenological shared reality rather than mere behavioral coordination.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

extensive experimental evidence for saying-is-believing memory shifts; studies showing audience tuning is modulated by epistemic authority of the audience, shared group membership, and relational motives. prior work established the behavioral phenomenon but debate remained about mechanisms and boundary conditions.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

shared reality in communication arises when: (1) a communicator evaluatively adapts a message to an audience's attitude, (2) the audience signals epistemic connection, and (3) the communicator experiences this as a validated inner state. this experience, not mere behavioral tuning, drives downstream memory and evaluative consequences.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

the synthesis implies: (1) audience tuning will create shared reality only when epistemic and relational motivations are engaged. (2) shared reality mediates the link between audience tuning and memory/evaluative shifts. (3) audience characteristics (epistemic authority, group membership) moderate shared reality creation.

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

n/a (review paper).

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

clarifies that audience tuning is necessary but not sufficient for shared reality: epistemic validation from the audience and relevant motivations must also be present. this addresses prior inconsistencies and generates testable boundary conditions.

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

shared reality in interpersonal communication is created through a motivated, experienced process of message tuning and epistemic validation — not simply through behavioral adaptation — and this experience has systematic downstream effects on communicators' memories and beliefs.
