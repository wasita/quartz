# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared experience, coattention, mentalizing, experience amplification, hedonic evaluation, implicit sharing

Boothby, Clark, and Bargh demonstrate that sharing an experience with another person — even silently and without explicit communication — amplifies the subjective intensity of that experience. both pleasant and unpleasant sensory experiences (chocolate tasting) were rated more intensely when a co-present person was simultaneously having the same experience.

full citation: [@boothby2014]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

does sharing an experience with another person change its intensity? prior research focused on explicit communication — the question of whether mere simultaneous engagement without words is sufficient to alter subjective experience had not been carefully isolated.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

shared attention research showed objects of joint attention receive greater cognitive resources and deeper processing. social facilitation literature examined how others' presence affects performance, but not subjective quality of experience. verbal social influence research required explicit communication.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

no prior work had demonstrated that non-communicative, simultaneous co-experience alone amplifies the hedonic valence of sensory experiences.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

when a stimulus is shared — meaning another person is simultaneously experiencing the same thing — it receives greater cognitive resources due to coattention processes, causing deeper processing and more intense subjective experience. this does not require communication or conscious awareness of social influence.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) pleasant shared experiences will be rated as more pleasant than the same experience when the co-present person is doing something else. (2) unpleasant shared experiences will be rated as more unpleasant under the same sharing condition.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

two laboratory experiments manipulating whether a co-present confederate was engaged in the same or different activity as the participant.

> ***what data is used?***

participant ratings of chocolate pleasantness/unpleasantness in shared vs. unshared conditions. the design held the presence of another person constant, varying only whether they were engaged in the same versus different activity.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

within-subjects experimental design. study 1: participants tasted pleasant chocolate simultaneously with a confederate who was either tasting the same chocolate or viewing an art book. study 2: same design with bitter/unpleasant chocolate.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

pleasant chocolate was rated as more pleasant in the shared condition. bitter chocolate was rated as more unpleasant in the shared condition. the effect occurred without any verbal interaction between participant and confederate.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

both hypotheses were confirmed. the amplification effect works in both hedonic directions, ruling out a simple social facilitation or positivity bias explanation.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

sharing an experience intensifies it in the direction of its hedonic valence, without communication. co-attention is the proposed mechanism — stimuli shared with others may automatically recruit greater attentional and cognitive resources. social amplification of experience is a basic, implicit feature of human perception.
