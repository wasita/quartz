# summary

>[!example] key terms
> common ground, presupposition, acceptance, context set, accommodation, propositional attitude, scorekeeping

Stalnaker offers a reductive account of common ground, defining it as a complex propositional attitude — specifically, mutual acceptance — built iteratively out of the individual attitudes of conversational participants. the paper clarifies the relationship between presupposition and common ground, introducing the notion of "acceptance" as broader than belief, and argues that the context of a conversation is best modeled as the set of propositions all participants mutually accept for the purposes of the conversation.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

the paper addresses a foundational question in the philosophy of language and pragmatics: what exactly is the common ground of a conversation, and how should it be formally characterized? common ground plays a central role in theories of presupposition, assertion, and communication — yet prior accounts left its structure underspecified, often conflating it with mutual belief.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

engages Stalnaker's own prior work on context sets (1970s-1990s), David Lewis's scorekeeping model of conversation, Gricean pragmatics treating conversational norms as grounded in rational cooperation, and Lewis's notion of common knowledge. Lewis's scorekeeping model is seen as too rigid and rule-governed, inconsistent with the Gricean picture of rational, flexible communication.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior accounts failed to distinguish between what is mutually believed and what is mutually accepted for conversational purposes. this matters because speakers often presuppose content that is not yet genuinely shared, and accommodation cannot be explained if common ground is simply equated with mutual belief.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

common ground should be defined in terms of iterated mutual acceptance rather than mutual belief. "acceptance" is broader than belief — it covers any attitude that functions as treating a proposition as true for the purposes of a given conversation. belief is the default value of acceptance in ordinary conversations, but acceptance can diverge from belief in contexts like games, thought experiments, or deliberation.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

this framework should account for: (1) how common ground can include propositions not actually commonly believed (e.g., after accommodation), (2) "defective contexts" where a speaker's presupposition is not yet shared, and (3) presupposition as a social attitude toward the common ground.

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

common ground is best understood as iterative mutual acceptance, not mutual belief. accommodation is a rational, pragmatic process by which common ground is updated to align with a speaker's presuppositional commitments, even when this creates a divergence between what is commonly accepted and commonly believed.
