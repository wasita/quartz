# summary

>[!example] key terms
> attitude similarity, interpersonal attraction, reinforcement, bogus stranger paradigm, similarity-attraction effect, proportion of similar attitudes

byrne and nelson use the bogus stranger paradigm to formalize the relationship between attitude similarity and interpersonal attraction. attraction is a positive linear function of the proportion of similar attitudes (y = 5.44x + 6.62), grounded in reinforcement theory.

full citation: [@byrne1965]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

why do people like some strangers and not others? formalizing the similarity-attraction relationship allows precise quantitative prediction.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

byrne and clore's reinforcement-affect model: similar attitudes provide positive reinforcement, associated with the source. balance theory (heider) also predicts attraction between similar others.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior work showed attitude similarity predicts attraction but confounded proportion with number of attitudes. this paper systematically varies proportion.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

attraction is a function of the proportion of positive reinforcements (similar attitudes), not the absolute number, and this relationship is linear.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) attraction is a positive linear function of proportion of similar attitudes. (2) proportion (not absolute number) is the operative variable.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

laboratory experiment using the bogus stranger paradigm; participants rated attraction to a stranger with manipulated proportions of agreement.

> ***what data is used?***

n=128 undergraduates rated a fictitious stranger whose attitudes showed varying proportions of agreement with participants' own.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

between-subjects experiment; proportion varied systematically; attraction measured via the interpersonal judgment scale; linear regression.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

attraction was a significant positive linear function of proportion (y = 5.44x + 6.62). proportion explained more variance than absolute number.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

both hypotheses confirmed. the reinforcement-affect model is supported.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

interpersonal attraction is a positive linear function of the proportion of similar attitudes, providing a precise quantitative law.
