# summary

>[!example] key terms
> social interaction, interacting minds, second-person neuroscience, hyperscanning, collective intelligence, dyadic cognition, naturalistic paradigms

wheatley et al. argue psychology has been limited by its focus on isolated individuals. the most important determinants of development, mental health, and cognition emerge from social interaction. the paper surveys emerging tools for studying interacting minds.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

human cognition is fundamentally shaped by social interaction. the dominant paradigm studies individuals in isolation, missing emergent properties of coupled minds.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

second-person neuroscience (schilbach et al.), distributed cognition, enactivism, and social neuroscience. neural synchrony has been overused as a proxy without adequate theoretical grounding.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

hyperscanning fMRI, EEG synchrony, and behavioral studies of joint action. hampered by difficulty capturing naturalistic interaction in labs.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

human perceptual, affective, and cognitive processes are shaped by interaction. brains distribute cognition and enable collective intelligence. psychology must study interacting nodes, not isolated individuals.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

theoretical/review paper. naturalistic paradigms combined with new tools will reveal emergent properties invisible to individual-level analysis.

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

a science of interacting minds requires new paradigms, tools, and training. the individual should be reconceptualized as embedded in social interaction.
