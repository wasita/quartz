# summary

>[!example] key terms
> perspective taking, common ground, referential communication, knowledge estimation, message formulation, audience design, egocentric bias

fussell and krauss investigated how speakers account for what they assume listeners know when formulating referring expressions in a referential communication task. speakers were reasonably accurate at estimating stimulus recognizability for others but showed an egocentric bias toward their own knowledge, and message informativeness varied inversely with perceived addressee familiarity — though effects were weaker than predicted.

full citation: [@fussell1992coordination]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

effective communication requires speakers to tailor messages to what listeners already know, yet people systematically differ in their knowledge. understanding how (and how well) speakers model others' knowledge is fundamental to theories of perspective taking, common ground, and interpersonal communication.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

builds on grice's cooperative principle and clark & marshall's common ground theory, which hold that speakers construct messages relative to shared knowledge. prior work by krauss and fussell highlighted the difficulty of perspective-taking under knowledge asymmetry. a gap existed in empirical documentation of how speakers' knowledge estimates actually shape message form.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior studies showed speakers adapt message length to perceived addressee knowledge and that people are often egocentric in estimating others' knowledge. however, direct evidence linking speakers' estimates of addressee knowledge to specific message properties (e.g., amount of information in referring expressions) was limited.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

speakers use estimates of addressee knowledge to calibrate the informativeness of referring expressions, but this process is imperfect because estimates are biased toward one's own knowledge level and because situational factors modulate the effect.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

(1) speakers' estimates of stimulus recognizability will predict message informativeness, with less information used when recognizability is perceived to be high. (2) estimates will be systematically biased toward the speaker's own knowledge of the stimulus.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

two-stage empirical study: stage 1 collected participants' estimates of how recognizable stimuli (famous people and everyday objects) were to others; stage 2 used a referential communication paradigm in which participants described the same stimuli to an addressee.

> ***what data is used?***

participant-generated recognizability estimates for a set of public figures and everyday objects, plus verbal referring expressions produced in the referential communication task; undergraduate samples.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

familiarity/recognizability ratings, referential communication task, content analysis of message length and informativeness, correlational analyses linking estimates to message properties.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

speakers were reasonably accurate at estimating stimulus recognizability but showed a bias toward their own knowledge. message informativeness varied inversely with estimated recognizability. effects were significant but smaller than anticipated, suggesting perspective-taking is partial.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

findings support the hypothesis that speakers calibrate messages to perceived addressee knowledge but qualify it: the egocentric bias in estimates and the attenuated effect size indicate speakers do not fully adopt the addressee's perspective, consistent with anchoring-and-adjustment models.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

speakers do adjust message content based on assumptions about addressee knowledge, but this adjustment is incomplete due to egocentric biases, suggesting that perfect common-ground coordination is the exception rather than the rule in everyday communication.
