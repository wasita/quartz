# summary

>[!example] key terms
> shared reality, close relationships, epistemic companions, relationship development, dyadic cognition, shared identity, sensemaking

rossignac-milon and higgins propose that close relationships are epistemic partnerships in which partners jointly satisfy needs to make sense of the world. they outline four cumulative phases through which shared reality deepens in close relationships: shared feelings, shared practices, shared coordination, and shared identity.

full citation: [@rossignac2018epistemic]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

most relationship science emphasizes affiliation, attachment, and interdependence, but the role of jointly constructing a shared understanding of the world has been underappreciated. specifying how epistemic companionship develops addresses a gap in theories of relationship formation and long-term intimacy.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

builds on higgins's shared reality theory and echterhoff et al. (2009), attachment theory, and social comparison theory. also draws on research on relationship-specific meaning-making and dyadic coping. existing relationship theories (e.g., interdependence theory) do not specifically account for the epistemic dimension.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

prior work showed shared reality effects in communicative dyads and the role of social validation in belief formation. research on couple cognition and transactive memory provides partial support. empirical evidence on the full developmental trajectory of epistemic companionship was limited at time of publication.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

close relationships are importantly constituted by epistemic companionship — the joint satisfaction of needs to understand the world — which progresses through four phases (shared feelings -> shared practices -> shared coordination -> shared identity), each building on and deepening the previous.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

the model implies: (1) relationship depth will correlate with the stage of shared reality reached. (2) epistemic validation from a partner will strengthen relationship commitment. (3) threats to shared reality (disagreement, invalidation) will undermine relationship satisfaction.

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

close relationships are fundamentally epistemic partnerships; partners become 'epistemic companions' who jointly construct and maintain shared reality across four developmental phases, and this shared-world building is central to relationship formation, deepening, and identity fusion.
