# summary

>[!example] key terms
> loneliness, social isolation, mortality, meta-analysis, public health, living alone, social relationships

holt-lunstad et al. meta-analyzed 70 studies showing loneliness, social isolation, and living alone each independently predict premature death, with effect sizes comparable to smoking and obesity.

full citation: [@holt-lunstad2015]

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

rising social isolation makes quantifying mortality risk essential. demonstrating that social deficits predict death justifies treating connection as a public health priority.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

stress-buffering and direct effects models of social support; evolutionary accounts. prior work had not distinguished subjective loneliness, objective isolation, and living alone as separate predictors.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

the 2010 holt-lunstad meta-analysis found 50% increased survival for adequate relationships. the 2015 paper extends by focusing on isolation and loneliness specifically.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

social isolation and loneliness are distinct but comparably sized risk factors for mortality, each deserving public health recognition.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

each will independently predict increased mortality after controlling for confounders.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

systematic review and meta-analysis; 70 prospective studies from 1980-2014.

> ***what data is used?***

70 studies reporting mortality as a function of loneliness, isolation, or living alone; varying follow-up periods.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

random-effects meta-analysis; moderator analyses for age, sex, health status, region; odds ratios after confound adjustment.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

social isolation OR = 1.29, loneliness OR = 1.26, living alone OR = 1.32. consistent across sex and region. more predictive in samples under age 65.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

both objective and subjective social deficits independently predict premature death at magnitudes comparable to established risk factors.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

loneliness, isolation, and living alone are significant mortality risk factors. social connectedness should be integrated into public health initiatives.
