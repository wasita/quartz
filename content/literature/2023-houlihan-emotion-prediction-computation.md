# summary

>[!example] key terms
> emotion prediction, appraisal theory, generative theory of mind, inverse planning, social preferences, prisoner's dilemma, computational model

houlihan et al. model emotion prediction as computation over a generative theory of mind: observers infer mental states via inverse planning, compute appraisals, then map to emotions. the model matches human predictions of 20 distinct emotions in a social dilemma context, with social preferences (equity, reputation) essential.

---

# topic and thesis

> ***briefly, why is the research question important to answer?***

predicting others' emotions from sparse event descriptions is a fundamental social-cognitive capacity. understanding the computational mechanisms has implications for empathy, social learning, and the cognition-affect interface.

---

# background

> ***what are the main theories in the literature? is there anything missing or problematic?***

appraisal theory (scherer, lazarus) and bayesian theory of mind. prior work lacked a unified model integrating inverse planning, appraisals, and discrete emotion concepts for social preferences.

> ***what is the empirical evidence? is there anything missing or problematic?***

humans make nuanced emotion predictions from minimal descriptions. prior models addressed inference or appraisal separately. the role of social preferences was computationally underexplored.

---

# argument and hypotheses

> ***does the article advance a new theoretical argument? if yes, what is it?***

emotion prediction chains inverse planning (inferring beliefs and preferences), appraisal (how mental states interact with outcomes), and mapping to emotions. social preferences are essential and irreducible.

> ***what are the hypotheses to be tested?***

the full model (with social preferences) will outperform a monetary-only model; social preferences contribute to nearly every emotion type.

---

# research design

> ***what is the methodological approach?***

computational modeling with behavioral validation; stimuli from a TV game show (golden balls) with a prisoner's dilemma structure.

> ***what data is used?***

human observer ratings of 20 emotions for contestants; predictions from full and ablated models.

> ***what are the specific methods and techniques?***

inverse planning over a generative model; appraisal computation; learned mapping to emotion labels; model comparison.

---

# results

> ***what are the key findings?***

the full model matched human predictions across all 20 emotions. monetary preferences alone were insufficient; social preferences were necessary for nearly every emotion.

> ***what do these findings say about the argument and hypotheses?***

emotion prediction is computation over a generative theory of mind; social preferences are irreducible.

---

# conclusion

> ***what is the overall conclusion?***

emotion prediction can be formalized as bayesian inference over mental states followed by appraisal, with social preferences playing a necessary role.
